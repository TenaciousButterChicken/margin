## Rules vs. examples — the central shift

This is the most important idea in the entire year.

**The metaphor.** Imagine you're trying to teach a friend to recognize a cat.

Old way (rules-based programming): you'd write a list. *"Has fur. Has four legs. Has whiskers. Says 'meow.' Tail under 14 inches. Eyes round and forward-facing…"* But every rule has an exception. Hairless cats. Three-legged cats. Kittens that haven't learned to meow. You'd be writing rules forever and you'd still get it wrong.

New way (machine learning): you show your friend ten thousand pictures of cats and ten thousand pictures of dogs, and just say "cat" or "dog" each time. After a while, they can tell new cats from new dogs without you ever having written down a single rule. They've **learned the pattern from examples**.

That second way is machine learning. The whole field is just figuring out clever ways to do that "show examples and let it figure out the pattern" trick on harder and harder problems.

<Callout variant="note">
**Where the math is taking us.** In Phase 2, we'll formalize this. "Show examples and figure out the pattern" will become: *we have data points <Math tex="(x_i, y_i)" />, and we want to find a function f such that <Math tex="f(x_i) \approx y_i" /> for as many i as possible.* That sentence is most of machine learning. We'll spend the year unpacking what "function," "close," and "as possible" mean precisely.
</Callout>

The point: students have been training ML systems with their behavior for years. Every time you skipped a song, watched a video to the end, or scrolled past a post, that was a labeled example feeding a model.

## Three flavors of ML — a teaser

We'll unpack each flavor in Session 2.

- **Supervised learning.** Learning from labeled examples ("this is a cat, this is a dog").
- **Unsupervised learning.** Finding patterns in unlabeled data.
- **Reinforcement learning.** Learning by trial and error with rewards.

Don't go deeper here. Plant the flag, move on.

Hit Shift+Enter. The output appears below.

**Step 3.** Add a new cell. We'll do something slightly more ML-shaped: load a real dataset and look at it.

```python
# Import pandas. Pandas is the standard library for working with
# spreadsheet-like data in Python. We rename it to "pd" because
# that's what every Python ML tutorial does.
import pandas as pd

# Load the famous iris dataset. This is a built-in dataset of flower
# measurements, often used as the "hello world" of machine learning.
from sklearn.datasets import load_iris
iris = load_iris(as_frame=True)
df = iris.frame

# Show the first 5 rows.
df.head()
```

When students run this, they see a small table: 5 rows, 5 columns. Sepal length, sepal width, petal length, petal width, target (the species).

*"That table is a dataset. Every row is one example (one flower). Every column is one feature (one measurement). The 'target' column is the label, which species the flower is. By the end of Phase 3, you will train a model on this exact dataset that can identify a flower's species from its measurements with 95+% accuracy."*

**Step 4.** One more cell, just to plant the visual:

```python
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Scatter plot: petal length vs petal width, colored by species.
plt.scatter(df['petal length (cm)'], df['petal width (cm)'],
            c=df['target'], cmap='viridis')
plt.xlabel('petal length (cm)')
plt.ylabel('petal width (cm)')
plt.title('Iris dataset: three species are visibly separable')
plt.show()
```

The output: three colored clusters in a 2D plot. *"Look at that. Three different species form three visible groups, just from two measurements. Your job by the end of the year: turn that visible separation into an algorithm a computer can use."*

<Activity title="AI or Real?" duration="20 min">

**Setup.** Prepare 10 images. Five are AI-generated (Midjourney, DALL·E, recent diffusion model output), five are real photos. Mix in some easy ones (obvious AI artifacts like extra fingers or melting backgrounds) and some genuinely hard ones.

**Format.** Project each image for 20 seconds. Students mark "AI" or "Real" on a worksheet, plus a 1 to 5 confidence rating and a one-line note ("looked off in the hands").

**The reveal.** Walk through each image. Ask volunteers to share their reasoning before revealing. Tally a class score for the room.

**The point.** Most classes score 5 to 7 out of 10. *"A computer made something that the most visually-trained human eyes in this room couldn't reliably distinguish from reality. That capability didn't exist five years ago. By the end of the year, you'll know how it works."*

## Likely student questions

<FAQ>
  <Q q="Is this just programming?">
No. In programming, you write the rules. In ML, you show examples and the system learns the rules.
  </Q>
  <Q q="Is AI going to take my job?">
Some jobs change a lot, some change a little, almost none are unchanged in 10 years. The students who understand how this stuff works will be the ones shaping that change instead of being shaped by it.
  </Q>
  <Q q="Is this going to be math-heavy?">
Yes, but the math is built up from algebra you already know. Each concept extends the previous one. We're going to derive the equations, not just hand them to you. If you can get to y = mx + b, you can get to ChatGPT, with enough sessions in between.
  </Q>
  <Q q="Is ML the same as AI?">
Loosely yes for this club. Strictly: AI is the goal, ML is the dominant approach to that goal.
  </Q>
