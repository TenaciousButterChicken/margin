There are two ways a computer can answer a question. The first is the way computers have answered questions for seventy years: someone wrote down a rule, and the computer follows it. The second is newer. Someone showed the computer a thousand examples and let it figure out the rule on its own. That second way is what this whole course is about.

## The thing you already know

You have been training models since you were two years old. The first time you saw a dog, someone said "dog." The second time, "dog." By the tenth time, you had built — somewhere in your head — a working theory of what dogs look like. Nobody handed you a list of features ("four legs, fur, tail, barks"). You inferred the pattern from examples.

Pattern inference from examples is exactly what machine learning is. The computer is the toddler. Our job is to be the parent pointing at the dog.

<Callout variant="note">
The technical word for this is **supervised learning** — supervised because we provide the labels. There are unsupervised flavors too (Session 14), and reinforcement flavors (not on the syllabus). But supervised is the workhorse.
</Callout>

## What today's activity is

The Lab opens with a small game called **AI or Real?** Ten images, one at a time. For each, you guess: was this generated by an AI, or is it a real photograph? After you guess, the platform tells you the answer and shows you what a public AI-detector model would have said about the same image.

The point of the activity is not to score well. The point is to see, viscerally, that the model already does this better than you do. We will spend the rest of the year understanding *how* it does it.

## Where this goes

Session 2 — you will train your own image classifier in a browser tab, on your own face, in fifteen minutes. Session 6 — you will build the optimization loop that makes any of this work, by hand, with a slider for the learning rate. Session 11 — you will build a neural network from scratch and watch it learn. Session 15 — you will see attention happen, in real time, in a small transformer.

The course is sixteen sessions. By the end, the entire stack is open to you. No black boxes.
