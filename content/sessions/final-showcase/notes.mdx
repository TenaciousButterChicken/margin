Each student presents one small project. The point isn't to dazzle. It's to see the year in retrospect: what we covered, what each student did, where they might go next.
</BigIdea>

<Callout variant="boss">
This is the end-of-year celebration. Invite faculty, parents, and friends. Order food.
</Callout>

**Format.** Each student gets 4 to 6 minutes: 3 minutes presentation, 1 to 2 minutes Q&A. Bring a soft timer. 16 students at 5 minutes each is 80 minutes; if you have a 60-minute meeting, do this over two sessions or run a longer event.

## Project scope (set this in Session 14)

Projects should be small. Goal: "I built and understood something," not "recreate AlphaGo."

Suggested formats:

- **A trained model.** Predicting club attendance from features. Predicting your next month's Spotify listening. Sentiment classifier for tweets.
- **A built application.** Web app or notebook demonstrating an ML concept. "Draw a digit, see what the model sees." An attention visualizer.
- **A presentation about a paper or system.** For students who don't want to code: pick a recent ML paper, present what it does, why it matters, what surprised them. AlphaFold. Anthropic's interpretability research.
- **An ethical analysis.** Pick an AI deployment in the news (a hiring system, court risk score, content moderation). Explain how it works, what's gone wrong, what they'd change.

In Session 14, students pitch project ideas. Two weeks before showcase, a 5-minute check-in. Students without a clear project get matched into pairs and given a starter idea.

## Student presentation template

Encourage roughly this structure for 3 minutes:

1. **The question (15 seconds).** "I wanted to know if I could predict X from Y."
2. **The data (30 seconds).** Where it came from, what it looks like, how big.
3. **The approach (45 seconds).** What model, what features, how trained.
4. **The result (45 seconds).** What worked, what didn't. A chart or two.
5. **What I learned (45 seconds).** One thing they want the room to take away.

Provide a slide template with these sections. Keep slide counts low (5 to 8 max).

## Teacher's job

Three things:

- **Time-keep ruthlessly.** Soft timer at 3 minutes, hard cutoff at 4. Otherwise the showcase runs long and loses momentum.
- **Ask one good question per student.** Pre-prep one based on their topic. Gives them confidence; gives audience something to learn.
- **Bridge between presentations.** A one-line connection. *"Saanvi just used logistic regression on a small dataset. Diego is going to use a neural network on a bigger one. Both came from Phases 2 and 4."*

## Year wrap (final 10 minutes)

After the last student, take 10 minutes for year-in-review. Show a slide with the 16 sessions. Walk through:

- *"Phase 1, we asked 'why care.' Look how far we've come."*
- *"Phase 2, we built our first model and derived gradient descent from y = mx + b. That algorithm is in every system you use now."*
- *"Phase 3, you derived sigmoid and log loss, implemented logistic regression from scratch, and built the Titanic project."*
- *"Phase 4, you derived backprop using the chain rule, implemented it in numpy, and trained a real neural network."*
- *"Phase 5, you saw transformers and attention. You can read 'Attention Is All You Need' now and follow it."*

End with what's next. Three concrete recommendations:

- **Andrew Ng's Machine Learning Specialization** on Coursera, for students who want the full math treatment.
- **Fast.ai's Practical Deep Learning for Coders**, for students who want code-first depth.
- **Build a project of your own.** Find a dataset that interests you. Try.

End with: *"Six months ago, most of you couldn't have explained 'machine learning.' Now you can derive its core algorithms, implement them in code, and explain what's inside ChatGPT. Whatever you do next, you understand the technology reshaping your world. That's a permanent change. Good luck."*

## Likely student questions during Q&A

Have a few prompts ready in case the room goes silent:

<FAQ>
  <Q q="Why did you pick this project?">
Open-ended. Lets the presenter tell their own story.
  </Q>
  <Q q="What was the hardest part?">
Reveals real learning more than the polished result.
  </Q>
  <Q q="If you had another month, what would you do next?">
Lets them dream a little.
  </Q>
  <Q q="What surprised you most?">
Often the best answer of the day.
  </Q>
  <Q q="What would you tell a 10th grader thinking about doing this?">
Recruits next year's club.
  </Q>
</FAQ>

## After the showcase

Three concrete artifacts:

1. **Class hall-of-fame document.** Each student's project title, one-paragraph summary, screenshot. Share with parents and school. Useful for next year's recruiting.
2. **Notes for next year.** What worked, what didn't, which sessions ran long, which activities landed. Future-you will thank present-you.
3. **A graduation message** to each student via email, acknowledging their specific contributions. 30 minutes of work; students remember years later.
